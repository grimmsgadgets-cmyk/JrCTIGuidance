Purpose #

This guide teaches junior CTI analysts how to do day-to-day adversary baselining work.

The goal is not to memorize actor names or copy vendor reports. The goal is to maintain a living answer to four questions:

  1. What does this actor normally do?
  2. What changed?
  3. Why might it have changed?
  4. What should defenders or stakeholders do differently?

Use this guide when asked to baseline adversary behavior, trace how actor tradecraft evolves, or translate actor activity into useful defensive actions.

Core Rule #

Do not start with attribution.

Start with observed behavior.

Bad:

Template
APT X is active again.

Better:

Template
A cluster associated with APT X is exploiting exposed edge devices against telecom and government targets. The behavior differs from its older phishing-heavy baseline. Confidence is moderate because reporting overlaps across two credible sources, but infrastructure evidence is limited.

The analyst's job is to move from:

Template
News item → observed behavior → baseline comparison → change assessment → defensive decision

What “Baseline Adversary Behavior” Means #

A baseline is the actor's normal operating pattern.

It is not a list of indicators. It is not a polished profile. It is not a vendor-report summary.

A useful baseline captures:

Question What to record
Who are they assessed to be? Actor, intrusion set, cluster, aliases, attribution confidence
Why do they operate? Espionage, disruption, theft, influence, extortion, access brokerage
Who do they target? Sectors, regions, technologies, victim types
How do they usually gain access? Phishing, vulnerability exploitation, stolen credentials, supply chain, insiders
What do they do after access? Discovery, credential theft, lateral movement, persistence, exfiltration, destruction
What tools and infrastructure recur? Malware, C2 patterns, hosting, domains, loaders, exploit chains
What behaviors are stable? Repeated TTPs, preferred timing, victimology, operational sequencing
What behaviors are changing? New targets, new tooling, new tempo, new destructive actions, new public messaging
What is the evidence quality? Confirmed, assessed, inferred, single-source, disputed

A good baseline allows you to recognize abnormality.

If you do not know the actor's normal, you cannot responsibly say something has changed.

Guiding Principles #

1. Track behavior before names #

Focus first on what was observed:

  • target set
  • initial access
  • tactics, techniques, and procedures
  • malware/tools
  • infrastructure
  • sequencing
  • evidence quality
  • confidence level

Actor names are useful, but they are unstable. Different vendors may use different names for overlapping activity. Treat names as labels, not truth.

2. Intelligence must support response #

A baseline is incomplete unless it helps someone make a better decision.

Every actor note should eventually answer:

  • What should SOC hunt for?
  • What should IR preserve or contain first?
  • What should vulnerability management prioritize?
  • What should executives understand?
  • What should legal or communications avoid overstating?

3. Not every actor matters equally #

Do not treat every actor report as equally urgent.

Assess:

Dimension Question
Intent Are they likely to target us, our sector, our geography, our customers, or our vendors?
Capability Can they execute the activity we are worried about?
Opportunity Do they have a plausible path into our environment or supply chain?
Activity Are they currently active or only historically relevant?
Impact What happens if they succeed?
Confidence How strong is the evidence?

4. MISP / OpenCTI-style principle: keep objects separate #

Do not mix the actor, campaign, malware, infrastructure, and report into one blob.

Keep these separate:

Object Meaning
Threat actor Who is believed to be behind activity
Intrusion set Observed cluster of activity / behavior
Campaign Time-bound operation
Malware/tool Capability used
Infrastructure Domains, IPs, certs, hosting, C2
Target Victim sector, region, organization, or technology
Report Source evidence
Relationship Uses, targets, attributed-to, indicates, similar-to

This prevents sloppy attribution and makes your work easier to update later.

Daily

Daily Workflow #

Daily timebox #

Recommended daily rhythm:

Time Task Output
30–45 minutes Collect new reporting Short intake notes
30 minutes Extract behavior and compare to baseline Updated actor card / change log
30–45 minutes Translate to stakeholders SOC, IR, VM, executive, or MSSP action notes

Do not spend the entire day reading. The value is in extraction, comparison, and decision support.

Step 1: Pick a Small Actor Set #

Do not track everyone.

Start with 3–5 actors or clusters.

Prioritize actors relevant to:

  • your sector
  • your region
  • your customers
  • your vendors
  • your technology stack
  • current geopolitical tensions
  • current exploitation trends
  • major ransomware / access broker ecosystems

Example weekly watch list:

Template
Tracked this week:
- Actor or cluster relevant to our sector
- Actor exploiting technology we use
- Ransomware / access broker ecosystem affecting our vertical
- State-aligned actor active during current geopolitical tension
- One learning-only actor for skill development

If you cannot explain why an actor is on the list, remove it.

Step 3: Collect New Reporting #

Use a fixed source list so you do not drift into random browsing.

Recommended source categories:

Source Type Examples
Government CISA, NSA, NCSC, CERTs
Vendor CTI Microsoft, Google / Mandiant, CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, Palo Alto Unit 42, Cisco Talos
Structured knowledge MITRE ATT&CK, MISP, OpenCTI
Cyber news The Record, BleepingComputer, CyberScoop, Dark Reading
Criminal ecosystem tracking ransomware.live, leak-site tracking, access broker reporting if available
Internal data SIEM alerts, EDR alerts, tickets, phishing reports, vulnerability exposure, incident cases

Daily intake template:

Template
# Daily Collection - [Date]

## New Items Found
1. [Source] reported [actor/cluster] targeting [sector/region/technology] using [method].
2. [Source] reported new malware/tool/infrastructure linked to [actor/cluster].
3. [Source] linked activity to [geopolitical event/conflict/election/sanctions/vulnerability].

## Relevant to Us?
Yes / No / Maybe

## Why?
Because we use [technology], operate in [sector], support [customer type], have [exposure], or are adjacent to [target region/business process].

Keep this short.

Template

Step 4: Convert Reports Into Behavior #

Do not copy summaries. Extract behavior.

For every useful report, fill this out:

Field Ask
Actor / cluster Who is reported? How confident is the label?
Victimology Who is being targeted?
Initial access How did they get in?
Execution What did they run?
Persistence How did they stay?
Privilege escalation Did they elevate?
Defense evasion What did they hide, disable, or bypass?
Credential access What credentials did they seek?
Discovery What did they enumerate?
Lateral movement How did they move?
Collection / exfiltration What did they collect or steal?
Impact Did they destroy, encrypt, leak, disrupt, or manipulate?
Objective Espionage, money, disruption, access sale, influence, prepositioning?
Evidence What proves it?
Confidence High, moderate, low, or unknown?

Step 5: Update the Baseline Only When Behavior Matters #

Do not update actor cards for every IOC.

Update the baseline when the report changes what you believe about:

  • who they target
  • how they get in
  • what they do after access
  • what they want
  • how urgent they are
  • what defenders should do
  • how confident attribution should be

Decision rule:

Template
If the item changes the actor's operating pattern, update the baseline.
If it only adds temporary indicators, record it as supporting evidence or send it to tooling.

Example update:

Template
## Recent Change
Date: [Date]

New reporting shows the actor exploiting exposed edge devices rather than relying mainly on phishing. This may represent either a true tradecraft shift or reporting bias from recent incidents.

Confidence: Moderate

Defensive implication:
Review internet-facing appliance exposure, VPN authentication logs, firewall logs, and recent patch status.

Weak update:

Template
Actor used IP address x.x.x.x.

That is usually not a baseline change.

Template

Step 6: Maintain a Normal vs. Changed Table #

This is the most important working artifact.

Template
# Actor Change Log

| Behavior Area | Baseline Normal | New Observation | Stable / Change / Unknown | Confidence | Defensive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Initial access | Spearphishing, valid accounts | Edge-device exploitation | Possible change | Moderate | Review VPN/firewall appliance logs |
| Targeting | Government / defense | Logistics / media | Expanded targeting | Moderate | Brief relevant business units |
| Objective | Espionage | Destructive staging | Possible escalation | Low / Moderate | Monitor for wipers and backup tampering |
| Tooling | Custom malware | LOLBins / RMM tools | Adaptation | High | Hunt for remote admin tool abuse |

This lets you brief change without rewriting full reports.

Step 7: Trace State and Non-State Actors Differently #

State-linked actor questions #

Ask:

Template
What geopolitical event might explain this activity?
Is the target set aligned to state interests?
Is this espionage, prepositioning, disruption, influence, or sabotage?
Is the actor becoming louder, faster, or more destructive?
Is attribution being overstated by media or vendors?
Could this be state-directed, state-tolerated, state-aligned, or merely opportunistic?

State-linked assessment template:

Template
## State-Linked Actor Assessment

Actor / cluster:
Relevant geopolitical context:
Observed target shift:
Observed tradecraft shift:
Likely objective:
Who benefits:
Confidence:
Caveats:

Assessment:
[Write 3–5 sentences. Separate observed facts from assessed meaning.]

Example:

Template
Assessment:
Recent activity may reflect conflict-driven collection against logistics and telecommunications. The behavior still resembles espionage more than destructive preparation. The target set aligns with state interests, but available reporting does not prove direct tasking. Confidence: moderate.

Non-state actor questions #

Ask:

Template
How are they making money?
Who are they partnering with?
Are they changing tools, affiliates, loaders, or targets?
Are they using geopolitical language as branding?
Are they actually state-aligned, or just opportunistic?
Did their business model change?

Non-state assessment template:

Template
## Non-State Actor Assessment

Actor / cluster:
Likely motivation:
Business model:
Observed target logic:
Tooling / affiliate changes:
Possible geopolitical opportunism:
Confidence:
Caveats:

Assessment:
[Write 3–5 sentences. Separate criminal motive, ideological messaging, and evidence of state alignment.]

Example:

Template
Assessment:
The group's messaging has become politically themed, but the tradecraft and victim selection still look financially motivated. The most likely explanation is criminal opportunism with ideological branding. Treat as state-aligned only if stronger evidence appears. Confidence: moderate.

State vs. Non-State Is Not Binary #

Do not force actors into clean categories.

Category Reality
State actor May use contractors, proxies, patriotic hackers, criminal tooling
Criminal actor May sell access to state actors or avoid certain targets for political reasons
Hacktivist May be authentic, state-amplified, or a front
Ransomware group May be financially motivated but geopolitically selective
Access broker May supply both criminals and state-linked operators

Better question:

Template
What behavior is observed, what motive best explains it, who benefits, what evidence supports attribution, and what changed from the prior baseline?
Template

Step 8: Translate the Baseline for Stakeholders #

Do not hand every stakeholder the same note.

Translate the same actor change into different action language.

SOC version #

Template
## SOC Note

Watch for:
- [Behavior 1]
- [Behavior 2]
- [Behavior 3]

Suggested hunt:
[One concrete hunt idea]

Useful logs:
- [Log source 1]
- [Log source 2]
- [Log source 3]

Example:

Template
Watch for:
- New VPN logins from unusual ASNs
- MFA reset requests followed by privileged access
- RMM tool installation
- Suspicious PowerShell discovery commands

Suggested hunt:
Find accounts with password reset or MFA reset followed by new device enrollment within 24 hours.

IR version #

Template
## IR Note

If this actor is suspected:
1. Prioritize [evidence source].
2. Preserve [critical logs].
3. Check [common next move].
4. Contain [likely persistence / access path].
5. Validate [exfiltration / impact indicator].

Example:

Template
If this actor is suspected:
1. Prioritize identity timeline.
2. Review help desk tickets.
3. Check privileged account changes.
4. Look for RMM installation.
5. Preserve SaaS audit logs early.

Vulnerability management version #

Template
## Vulnerability Management Note

Relevant exposure:
- [Product / technology]
- [Exploit status]
- [Known actor relevance]

Recommended action:
1. Confirm exposure.
2. Prioritize patching or mitigation.
3. Validate compensating controls.
4. Report unresolved exposure.

Executive version #

Template
## Executive Note

Recent reporting suggests [actor/cluster] is shifting toward [behavior].

Business risk:
[Plain-English risk]

Recommended action:
[One to three practical actions]

Confidence:
[High / Moderate / Low]

Example:

Template
Recent reporting suggests increased targeting of identity and help desk workflows.

Business risk:
The main risk is unauthorized access through support-process abuse, not malware alone.

Recommended action:
Review help desk verification, MFA reset procedures, and privileged access monitoring.

Confidence:
Moderate.

Legal / communications version #

Template
## Legal / Communications Note

Do say:
- Observed activity is consistent with [behavior / cluster] based on [evidence].
- Attribution confidence is [level].
- Defensive steps are underway.

Do not say:
- A government definitely did this unless evidence supports it.
- The actor is confirmed if reporting is only circumstantial.
- The organization is safe because indicators were blocked.

Quality Checklist Before You Send Work #

Before you hand off your note, check:

Check Pass?
Did I separate observed facts from assessment?
Did I include confidence language?
Did I compare new behavior against baseline normal?
Did I avoid overclaiming attribution?
Did I avoid dumping IOCs without context?
Did I translate the finding for at least one stakeholder?
Did I identify what evidence would change my assessment?
Did I record open questions?

If most boxes are empty, the work is not CTI yet. It is just note-taking.

Warning

Common Failure Modes #

Failure Mode 1: Actor-name obsession #

Weak:

Template
APT X is active.

Better:

Template
A cluster associated with APT X is targeting exposed appliances in sectors relevant to us. The behavior may indicate a shift in initial access. Confidence is moderate.

Failure Mode 2: IOC dumping #

Weak:

Template
Here are 50 IP addresses.

Better:

Template
The actor is using short-lived VPS infrastructure for C2. Blocking the listed IPs may help tactically, but defenders should also hunt for beaconing patterns, unusual outbound connections, and related process behavior.

Failure Mode 3: No stakeholder translation #

Weak:

Template
The actor uses valid accounts.

Better:

Template
Because the actor commonly abuses valid accounts, SOC should review abnormal login behavior, MFA reset events, privileged access changes, and new device enrollments.

Failure Mode 4: Treating reporting as proof #

Weak:

Template
This is definitely state-directed.

Better:

Template
The activity aligns with state interests and overlaps with previously reported tradecraft, but available evidence does not prove direct state tasking. Confidence: moderate.

Failure Mode 5: No baseline comparison #

Weak:

Template
The actor used a new malware family.

Better:

Template
The actor historically relied on credential theft and living-off-the-land execution. New reporting of custom malware may represent a tooling expansion, but more evidence is needed before treating it as a durable tradecraft shift.

Appendix A: Reference Sources #

These references support the workflow and terminology used in this guide.

MITRE ATT&CK #

MITRE ATT&CK is useful for recording adversary behavior using tactics and techniques based on observed activity. ATT&CK group pages are useful, but analysts should treat group names carefully because naming and scope can differ across organizations.

References:

  • MITRE ATT&CK Groups

https://attack.mitre.org/groups/

  • Getting Started with ATT&CK: Threat Intelligence

https://medium.com/mitre-attack/getting-started-with-mitre-att-ck-threat-intelligence-4eb205be4b2f

OpenCTI #

OpenCTI helps structure CTI by separating threats, intrusion sets, campaigns, malware/tools, infrastructure, sectors, attack patterns, and relationships.

References:

  • OpenCTI Threats Documentation

https://docs.opencti.io/latest/usage/exploring-threats/

  • OpenCTI Usage Overview

https://docs.opencti.io/latest/usage/overview/

MISP Galaxy #

MISP Galaxy provides a way to represent larger CTI entities such as threat actors, tools, ransomware, and ATT&CK matrices as clusters that can be linked to events or attributes.

References:

  • MISP Galaxy

https://misp-galaxy.org/

  • MISP Project Features

https://www.misp-project.org/features/

  • CIRCL MISP Galaxy User Guide

https://www.circl.lu/doc/misp/galaxy/

Intelligence-Driven Incident Response #

Intelligence-Driven Incident Response frames CTI as understanding adversaries, their capabilities, motivations, goals, and how they use cyber operations to accomplish those goals. The key operational lesson for juniors: intelligence should help incident response and defense teams act faster and with better context.

References:

  • O'Reilly / book listing

https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/intelligence-driven-incident-response/9781098120675/

Attribution and ATT&CK #

Public CTI material on attribution and ATT&CK is frequently useful for teaching juniors to be careful with attribution, actor names, and behavior-based analysis.

References:

  • Red Canary: The Attribution Game: When Knowing Your Adversary Matters

https://redcanary.com/blog/opinions-insights/apt-attribution-rsa/

Appendix B: Short Version for Junior Analysts #

Every day, answer this:

Template
What changed from this actor's normal behavior, how confident am I, and what should someone do about it?

Use this sequence:

Template
1. Pick one actor.
2. Read only relevant reporting.
3. Extract behavior, not prose.
4. Compare behavior to the baseline.
5. Mark stable, changed, or unknown.
6. Add confidence.
7. Translate into action for SOC, IR, VM, executives, or customers.
8. Record open questions.

If your note does not include behavior, confidence, baseline comparison, and action, it is not done.
